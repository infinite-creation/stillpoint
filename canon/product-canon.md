## Canon statement (official)
Still Point is a soft, external, nose-worn breath-awareness amplifier. It anchors on the outer nasal sidewalls via S-curved arms and intentionally floats around the nasal tip, while its lower span — the sill — hovers nearly flush against the nasal floor just below the nostrils. This geometry gently redirects airflow, creating converging streams and vortices beneath the nostrils on inhalation, and rebounding warm plumes along the sides of the nose on exhalation. The result: amplified breath sensation across the full nostril surface on every breath cycle, making the breath more vivid and easier to focus on and return to.

---

## Non-negotiables (never break these)
- **External-only:** never enters the nostrils.
- **Full breath volume:** redirects airflow but never reduces it. Breathing remains full and unrestricted.
- **Primary anchor = sidewalls:** stability comes from the alar sidewalls, not the tip.
- **Tip float zone:** the tip is intentionally not clamped; space is part of the function.
- **Gentle retention:** resting, not pinching.
- **Instant usability:** on/off in seconds; no setup.
- **Practice-first:** supports attention; does not demand attention.

## Structural elements (named components)

### The sill (copy term) / span (technical term)
The flat structural element that bridges beneath both nostrils. It is not an arch or open channel — it is a solid span that hovers nearly flush against the nasal floor, creating a narrow airspace between itself and the base of the nostrils. A slight V-shape at the center accommodates the apex (the anatomical structure separating the two nostrils, which sits slightly lower than the nostril openings), allowing the sill to contour closely to the nasal floor on both sides.

- **Sill** — preferred term in consumer-facing and brand copy. Warm, architectural, minimal. Quietly echoes "Still."
- **Span** — preferred term in technical, canon, and explanatory copy. Precise and unambiguous.

### The S-curve arms
The arms are not straight — each follows a deliberate S-curve from the sill upward to the sidewall contact point. This curve serves two distinct functions:
1. **Fit:** the S-curve follows the natural narrowing of the nose from the face outward, making full contact along the outer alar sidewall for a secure, contour-based fit.
2. **Exhalation channeling:** the inward curve of each arm actively catches and directs warm exhaled air upward along the sides of the nose, engaging more of the nostril surface on the out-breath and amplifying exhalation sensation.

### The tip float zone
The nasal tip remains completely free — floating above the top of the U-frame. This is intentional and functional: the space allows airflow to wrap around the front of the nose and contributes to the front entry path on inhalation.

## Fit + contact model (standard language)
- **Primary anchor:** outer nostril wings / alar sidewalls (left/right), via S-curved arm contact.
- **Float zone:** nasal tip remains free above the top of the frame; space is part of the airflow mechanism.
- **Sill position:** spans beneath both nostrils, hovering nearly flush against the nasal floor. Proximity to the nasal floor determines amplification level.
- **Apex accommodation:** slight V at the sill center clears the apex, allowing the sill to sit close on both sides.
- **Lower clearance:** sill sits just above the philtrum/upper lip line; minimal to no interference with speech or expression.

## Mechanism of action (full, canon)

### Proximity as the amplification dial
The closer the sill sits to the nasal floor, the more completely it redirects incoming airflow, and the greater the amplification. The device can be adjusted by the user — pulling it slightly down widens the gap between sill and nostrils, decreasing amplification. Bringing it closer narrows the gap and increases it. Proximity is the dial.

### Inhalation mechanism
The sill hovers nearly flush beneath the nostrils, closing off the most direct upward air path. Incoming air is gently redirected along two alternative routes:
- **Back-bottom path:** air enters from behind, where the base of the nose meets the upper lip.
- **Front path:** air enters from the front, passing around the tip of the nose and through the open tip float zone.

These two streams converge beneath the nostrils and create **vortices** — swirling airflow patterns that further amplify sensory stimulation beyond simple surface contact. The combined effect washes across more of the nostril's surface area, particularly the sides and lower portions where breath sensation is richest.

### Exhalation mechanism
Warm air exits the nostrils downward and immediately contacts the inner surface of the sill. It rebounds outward in three directions: out the back, out the front, and upward along the sides of the nose — channeled by the inward curve of the S-curve arms. These rising **plumes** of warm air engage the sides and upper nostril surface, creating a second wave of amplified sensation on the out-breath.

### Bidirectional sensory experience
Every breath cycle is amplified in both directions:
- **Inhalation:** cool air, redirected from two paths, converging in vortices beneath the nostrils.
- **Exhalation:** warm air, rebounding off the sill, rising as plumes along the sides of the nose.

The breath doesn't change in volume — it changes in vividness.

## What users should feel (experience targets)
- More vivid breath sensation at the nostrils on both inhalation and exhalation.
- A coolness on the in-breath across more of the nostril surface than usual.
- A warmth on the out-breath as air rises along the sides of the nose.
- Faster reorientation when the mind drifts.
- A "quieting" effect through steadier attention.
- Comfort that remains stable over time.

---

## What it is not (avoid confusion)
- Not a nasal insert.
- Not a nasal dilator.
- Not a medical breathing aid.
- Not a tracker, wearable tech, or performance device.
- Not a device that reduces breath volume or restricts airflow.

- Minimal visual noise; sculptural simplicity; friendly and non-clinical.

## Purpose
This document defines what we **can say**, what we **should avoid**, and the preferred **safe phrasing** for Still Point across website, ads, packaging, emails, PR, and agent-generated copy.

The goal: communicate benefits clearly **without medical claims**, overpromises, or wording that implies diagnosis/treatment/cure.

---

## Core positioning (safe, canonical)
Still Point is a soft, external, nose-worn **breath anchor** that amplifies the felt signal of breathing at the nostrils. It anchors on the outer nasal sidewalls via S-curved arms while floating around the nasal tip. Its lower span — the sill — hovers nearly flush against the nasal floor, gently redirecting airflow to create richer, more vivid breath sensation on every inhale and exhale. Full breath volume is always preserved.

### 5) Mechanism description (product truth — use accurately)
This is a new claim zone added in v2.0. The airflow-redirection mechanism is a genuine, accurate product truth and can be described in copy — but must be framed as **experience and sensation**, not physiology or medical mechanism.

**Safe mechanism claims**
- "Gently redirects airflow to make the breath more vivid."
- "The breath doesn't change in volume — it changes in vividness."
- "Incoming air finds two paths, washing across more of the nostril's surface."
- "Cool on the in-breath. Warm on the out-breath. Every cycle amplified."
- "More of the nostril comes alive with each breath."
- "The sill hovers just below the nostrils, redirecting air to where you feel it most."
- "Amplifies breath sensation in both directions — in and out."

**Safe experiential descriptions of the vortex (use selectively)**
The converging air streams create swirling patterns beneath the nostrils that deepen breath sensation. In copy, frame this as what the user *feels*, not as a clinical mechanism.
- ✅ "Air arrives from two directions, meeting beneath the nostrils in a way that makes the breath feel fuller and more present."
- ✅ "The sensation is richer than a single stream — more surface, more feeling, more to return to."
- ❌ "Creates vortices that stimulate nasal neurons." ← too clinical, implies medical mechanism

**Safe experiential descriptions of exhalation plumes (use selectively)**
The warm air rebounding off the sill and rising along the sides is a real, felt experience. Frame as sensation.
- ✅ "On the out-breath, warmth rises along the sides of the nose — a quiet signal that the breath has been and gone."
- ✅ "Each exhale is as present as each inhale."
- ❌ "Exhalation plumes stimulate the nasal sidewalls." ← too clinical

**Rule for mechanism copy:** If the sentence describes *what the user feels*, it's likely safe. If it describes *what the body is doing biologically*, slow down and reframe.

## Soft claims (yellow light — use carefully with qualifiers)
These are allowed **only** when framed as experience, not treatment.

- ~~"Stress relief"~~ → ✅ **moved to green-light in v2.1.** Use freely.
- "Stress support" / "helps you downshift" remain good alternatives when a softer tone is preferred.
- "Anxiety" → prefer "anxious feelings" / "when you feel anxious" / "when you're activated."
- "Insomnia" → prefer "difficulty falling asleep" / "trouble returning to sleep."
- "Regulates the nervous system" → prefer "supports nervous system settling."
- "Vortex" → accurate but technical; prefer experiential framing in consumer copy.
- "Plumes" → evocative and safe in longer, more poetic copy; avoid in headlines or quick claims.

**Rule:** If a sentence sounds like a medical promise, rewrite it as **support + experience + qualifier**.

## Avoid list (red light)
Do not use these in marketing copy, ads, or packaging unless you explicitly choose a regulated/clinical pathway.

### Medical / diagnostic / treatment language
- "Treats anxiety / depression / PTSD / insomnia"
- "Cures" / "heals" / "prevents" (medical context)
- "Therapy device" / "medical device" / "clinically proven" (unless you actually have substantiation)
- "Prescription alternative"
- "Stops panic attacks"
- "Fixes breathing" / "improves oxygen" / "opens your airway"
- "Nasal dilator" / "improves airflow" (unless you intend to claim that and can support it)

### Neurological / physiological overclaims
- "Stimulates nasal neurons" / "activates neurons" ← frame as sensation instead
- "Stimulates vagus nerve" (unless you have substantiation and are prepared to defend it)
- "Increases dopamine/serotonin"
- "Rewires your brain"
- "Activates parasympathetic system" (unless framed softly and carefully)
- "Creates vortices that stimulate…" ← describe the felt experience instead

### Guaranteed outcomes / time promises
- "Guaranteed calm"
- "Works instantly"
- "Fall asleep in 1 minute"
- "Never lose focus again"

### Retired phrases (do not use — replaced in v2.0)
- **"Leaves airflow unobstructed"** ← RETIRED. Inaccurate — the device redirects airflow. Replace with "gently redirects airflow" or "full breath volume, redirected path."
- **"Airflow completely unobstructed"** ← RETIRED. Same reason.

## Safe phrasing patterns (copy formulas)

### Formula A — Designed to support (most reusable)
"Still Point is designed to support **[experience]** by **[mechanism]**."
- Example: "Still Point is designed to support steadier attention by making the breath easier to feel at the nostrils."

### Formula B — Many people find (social proof without overclaim)
"Many people find **[benefit]** when **[context]**."
- Example: "Many people find it easier to return to the breath when their mind drifts."

### Formula C — When you feel X (emotional honesty, no diagnosis)
"When you feel **[state]**, Still Point can help you **[non-medical action]**."
- Example: "When you feel scattered, Still Point can help you reconnect with the breath."

### Formula D — Sleep support (non-clinical)
"At night, it can be a helpful companion for **winding down** and **returning to sleep** by giving attention an easy sensory place to land."

### Formula E — Mechanism as experience (new in v2.0)
"Rather than **[what you'd expect]**, Still Point **[what actually happens]** — so the breath **[felt result]**."
- Example: "Rather than simply resting on the nose, Still Point gently redirects airflow — so the breath feels fuller, more present, easier to stay with."

### Formula F — Volume vs. vividness (new in v2.0)
"The breath doesn't change in **[volume / effort / pace]** — it changes in **[vividness / presence / clarity]**."
- Example: "The breath doesn't change in volume — it changes in vividness."

### Formula G — Bidirectional (new in v2.0)
"**[In-breath quality]** on the inhale. **[Out-breath quality]** on the exhale. Every breath, amplified."
- Example: "Cool and present on the inhale. Warm and rising on the exhale. Every breath, amplified."

## Approved adjectives (brand-safe)
Quiet, gentle, steady, grounding, supportive, calming, tactile, simple, discreet, embodied, minimal, reliable, portable, vivid, present, warm, cool, bidirectional.

## Words to be cautious with
Relief, therapy, treatment, cure, clinical, regulate, fix, heal, insomnia, anxiety (use only with soft framing). Vortex, plumes, neuronal (accurate internally; reframe experientially in consumer copy).

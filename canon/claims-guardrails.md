## Purpose
This document defines what we **can say**, what we **should avoid**, and the preferred **safe phrasing** for Still Point across website, ads, packaging, emails, PR, and agent-generated copy.

The goal: communicate benefits clearly **without medical claims**, overpromises, or wording that implies diagnosis/treatment/cure.

---

## Core positioning (safe, canonical)
Still Point is a soft, external, nose-worn **breath anchor** that amplifies the felt signal of breathing at the nostrils. **It rests securely on the outer nasal sidewalls while floating around the nasal tip**, leaving airflow unobstructed and helping attention return naturally through sensation.

---

## Allowed claim zones (green light)

### 1) Attention + meditation support (primary)
**Safe claims**
- “Helps make the breath easier to feel.”
- “Helps attention return to the breath.”
- “Supports sustained concentration during meditation.”
- “A tactile anchor for breath awareness.”
- “Amplifies the felt signal of breathing at the nostrils.”
- “Helps set you up for a deeper session by making the breath easier to feel and return to.”
- “Helps cultivate and strengthen focused attention over time.”

**Safe outcome language**
- “Many people find it easier to stay with the breath.”
- “Designed to support steadier attention.”
- “Helps you return—again and again—without forcing.”


### 2) Calm + nervous-system settling (supportive, non-medical)
**Safe claims**
- “Supports a sense of calm.”
- “Helps you settle between tasks.”
- “A grounding cue when you feel scattered.”
- “Supports nervous system settling through breath-based attention.”

**Safe qualifiers**
- “May help…”
- “Designed to support…”
- “Many users report…”
- “Can be a helpful companion for…”
- "Helps cultivate…”

### 3) Sleep support (supportive, non-medical)
**Safe claims**
- “Sleep support: helps you fall asleep more easily and return to sleep if you wake in the night with a busy mind.”
- “A calming breath anchor for bedtime.”
- “Helps you reconnect with breath sensation when the mind is active at night.”

**Better phrasing (preferred)**
- “Support for winding down and returning to sleep.”
- “Designed to help the breath feel easier to follow at bedtime.”

### 4) Portability + simplicity (product truth)
**Safe claims**
- “No apps. No tracking. No batteries.”
- “On/off in seconds.”
- “Discreet, travel-friendly.”
- “Leaves airflow unobstructed.”
- “Worn externally; never enters the nostrils.”

## Soft claims (yellow light — use carefully with qualifiers)
These are allowed **only** when framed as experience, not treatment.

- “Stress relief” → prefer “stress support” / “helps you downshift.”
- “Anxiety” → prefer “anxious feelings” / “when you feel anxious” / “when you’re activated.”
- “Insomnia” → prefer “difficulty falling asleep” / “trouble returning to sleep.”
- “Regulates the nervous system” → prefer “supports nervous system settling.”

**Rule:** If a sentence sounds like a medical promise, rewrite it as **support + experience + qualifier**.

---

## Avoid list (red light)
Do not use these in marketing copy, ads, or packaging unless you explicitly choose a regulated/clinical pathway.

### Medical / diagnostic / treatment language
- “Treats anxiety / depression / PTSD / insomnia”
- “Cures” / “heals” / “prevents” (medical context)
- “Therapy device” / “medical device” / “clinically proven” (unless you actually have substantiation)
- “Prescription alternative”
- “Stops panic attacks”
- “Fixes breathing” / “improves oxygen” / “opens your airway”
- “Nasal dilator” / “improves airflow” (unless you intend to claim that and can support it)

### Guaranteed outcomes / time promises
- “Guaranteed calm”
- “Works instantly”
- “Fall asleep in 1 minute”
- “Never lose focus again”

### Overreaching neuroscience/physiology claims
- “Stimulates vagus nerve” (unless you have substantiation and are prepared to defend it)
- “Increases dopamine/serotonin”
- “Rewires your brain”
- “Activates parasympathetic system” (unless framed softly and carefully)

## Safe phrasing patterns (copy formulas)

### Formula A — Designed to support (most reusable)
“Still Point is designed to support **[experience]** by **[mechanism]**.”
- Example: “Still Point is designed to support steadier attention by making the breath easier to feel at the nostrils.”

### Formula B — Many people find (social proof without overclaim)
“Many people find **[benefit]** when **[context]**.”
- Example: “Many people find it easier to return to the breath when their mind drifts.”

### Formula C — When you feel X (emotional honesty, no diagnosis)
“When you feel **[state]**, Still Point can help you **[non-medical action]**.”
- Example: “When you feel scattered, Still Point can help you reconnect with the breath.”

### Formula D — Sleep support (non-clinical)
“At night, it can be a helpful companion for **winding down** and **returning to sleep** by giving attention an easy sensory place to land.”

## Approved adjectives (brand-safe)
Quiet, gentle, steady, grounding, supportive, calming, tactile, simple, discreet, embodied, minimal, reliable, portable.

## Words to be cautious with
Relief, therapy, treatment, cure, clinical, regulate, fix, heal, insomnia, anxiety (use only with soft framing).

---

## Optional disclaimer language (use where appropriate)
- “Still Point is not a medical device and is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease.”
- “Individual experiences vary.”

(Use on packaging/footer or where you want extra protection. Not always needed on every page.)
